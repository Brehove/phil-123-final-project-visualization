## **What You'll Create**

The Signature Assignment: Integrative Learning is your capstone project for PHIL 123\. It's designed to do two things:

### **1\. Practice Philosophy as a Tool for Building**

Throughout this course, you've engaged with ethical frameworks and philosophical concepts. This project asks you to use these ideas as tools for thinking about real decisions and to create something that addresses a real need in a context you care about.

The question at the heart of this project is one that all [philosopher builders](https://blog.cosmos-institute.org/p/the-philosopher-builder) must answer: **What should I build, and why?**

Whether you design a tool, organize an event, or produce content for a broader audience, you'll practice translating philosophical thinking into concrete action. You'll consider tradeoffs, identify stakeholders, and make design decisions grounded in ethical reasoning. The goal is both to *understand* how ethical frameworks inform design and *apply* it in ways that could impact your field or community.

### **2\. Demonstrate Integrative Learning**

This project also demonstrates **integrative learning**. This type of learning connects:

* Your experiences with AI tools  
* Ethical theories and contemporary debates from the course  
* A real-world context where AI matters  
* Clear communication to a specific audience

You'll choose one of two pathways based on your interests and goals, then develop your project through a structured design process across Modules 8-16.

## **Your Journey at a Glance**

You won't do this all at once. Over the next nine modules, you'll explore three AI ethics "zones" through Deep Research, then define a real problem you want to address. From there, you'll develop your concept, build it out, get feedback from peers, and polish your final deliverable. By Module 16, you'll have created something you can show to others, and you'll understand the ethical reasoning behind every choice you made.

## **Double Diamond Framework**

This project uses the [Double Diamond](https://www.designcouncil.org.uk/our-resources/the-double-diamond/) design framework developed by the UK Design Council in 2003\. The Double Diamond is a simple way to visualize the design process. It works for any creative project, regardless of the specific tools or methods you use.

### **Why "Double Diamond"?**

The shape comes from how creative work actually happens. Each diamond represents a cycle of opening up (exploring possibilities) and then narrowing down (making decisions). You do this twice: once to understand the problem, and once to build the solution.

* **Divergent thinking** \= opening up, exploring broadly, generating options  
* **Convergent thinking** \= narrowing down, synthesizing, making choices

The first diamond helps you figure out *what problem to solve*. The second diamond helps you figure out *how to solve it*.

#### **First Diamond: Problem Space**

**Discover:** You start by exploring broadly. What's happening in this space? What do people actually experience? What does the research say? You're not trying to solve anything yet; you're trying to understand the landscape. In this project, your Deep Research (Modules 8-10) is your discovery phase.

**Define:** Now you narrow down. What specific problem emerged from your research? Who is affected? What's actually at stake? You synthesize everything you learned into a clear, actionable problem statement. Your Problem Brief (Module 11\) is where you define.

#### **Second Diamond: Solution Space**

**Develop:** With a clear problem defined, you open up again. What are different ways you could address this? You brainstorm multiple concepts, weigh their strengths and weaknesses, and then commit to one direction. Your Concept Document (Module 12\) captures this exploration and commitment.

**Deliver:** Finally, you build, test, and refine. You create drafts, get feedback, iterate, and polish until you have something ready to share. Modules 13-16 take you through specification, drafting, revision, and final delivery.

## **Examples Paths**

### **Example 1: Philosopher-Builder Pathway**

*Alex is a nursing student who's skeptical about AI in healthcare but curious about its potential.*

During **Deep Research** (Modules 8-10), Alex explored how algorithmic bias affects diagnostic tools, how automation is changing nursing workflows, and how AI is being used to generate patient communication. She noticed a gap: rural nurses weren't getting practical training on when to trust (and when to question) AI recommendations.

In her **Problem Brief** (Module 11), Alex defined her audience as nurse educators at community colleges and articulated success criteria focused on building critical thinking, not just technical skills. Her **Concept Document** (Module 12\) explored three options (a workshop series, a simulation game, and a training module) before committing to a 5-part training curriculum that balanced efficiency with patient autonomy.

Her **Project Spec** (Module 13\) detailed learning objectives, activities, and facilitator guides, drawing on Effective Altruism's focus on measurable impact and transhumanist debates about human-AI collaboration. Alex's **First Draft** (Module 14\) included three complete modules; peer feedback helped her simplify the language for non-technical audiences in her **Revised Draft** (Module 15). Her **Final Project** was a polished curriculum packet with facilitator notes, ready to pilot at her local community college.

### **Example 2: Public Philosophy Pathway**

*Jordan is a communications major who's watched his younger siblings fall for AI-generated misinformation and wants to do something about it.*

Jordan's **Deep Research** (Modules 8-10) uncovered how recommendation algorithms amplify sensational content, how automated content farms operate, and how AI image generators are making fakery easier than ever. He realized that teens don't have accessible, engaging resources for spotting AI-generated content.

In his **Problem Brief** (Module 11), Jordan targeted high school students and defined success as "making media literacy feel relevant, not preachy." His **Concept Document** (Module 12\) weighed a TikTok series, a zine, and an interactive website before committing to a 2-episode podcast mini-series. The longer format allowed for nuance and guest interviews.

Jordan's **Project Spec** (Module 13\) outlined episode topics, guest possibilities, and a distribution plan, informed by Nietzsche's critique of passive consumption and accelerationist ideas about technology outpacing our ability to adapt. His **First Draft** (Module 14\) included two recorded episodes; peers suggested adding more concrete "try this" takeaways, which Jordan incorporated into his **Revised Draft** (Module 15). His **Final Project** was a mini podcast series with episode transcripts and a discussion guide for teachers.

## **Milestone Deliverables**

Each milestone builds on the previous one. Templates are provided for each.

### **Module 11: Problem Brief**

Define what you're solving and for whom.

* Context summary (synthesis of your research)  
* Problem statement (specific and actionable)  
* Target audience (who needs this)  
* Audience needs (what they're missing)  
* Success criteria (how you'll know it works)  
* Pathway declaration

*Example: Alex identified that rural nurse educators lack practical AI training resources; Jordan defined their audience as high schoolers who need media literacy that "feels relevant, not preachy."*

### **Module 12: Concept Document**

Explore how you might solve the problem.

* 2-3 concept options with strengths/weaknesses  
* Commitment to one concept with rationale  
* Value alignment  
* Visual or draft element

*Example: Alex weighed a workshop series, simulation game, and training curriculum before committing to the curriculum; Jordan considered TikTok, a zine, and a podcast before choosing the podcast for its depth.*

### **Module 13: Project Spec**

Plan exactly what you'll build.

* Detailed outline (pathway-specific)  
* Design decisions with rationale  
* Stakeholder analysis  
* Timeline and resources  
* Risks and contingencies

*Example: Alex's spec detailed 5 training modules with learning objectives and facilitator guides; Jordan outlined 4 podcast episodes with guest possibilities and a distribution plan.*

### **Module 14: First Draft**

Create a working version.

* Complete draft of your project  
* Self-assessment against success criteria  
* Feedback questions for peer reviewers

*Example: Alex submitted three complete training modules; Jordan recorded two full podcast episodes.*

### **Module 15: Revised Draft**

Improve based on feedback.

* Peer reviews given (2 classmates)  
* Revision notes (what you changed and why)  
* Updated draft

*Example: Peer feedback helped Alex simplify language for non-technical audiences; Jordan added more concrete "try this" takeaways based on reviewer suggestions.*

### **Module 16: Final Project**

Polish and reflect.

* Final Artifact (Canvas)  
* Project Documentation (Canvas)

*Example: Alex delivered a polished curriculum packet with facilitator notes; Jordan submitted a complete podcast series with transcripts and a discussion guide.*

### **2\. Project Documentation**

This is a reflective essay written for a general audience. Someone unfamiliar with your project should be able to read this document and understand what you created, why it matters, and how you partnered with AI throughout the process. Write in first person and use clear, accessible language.

**Use the following section headings:**

#### **The Problem I Addressed**

Explain the problem you set out to solve. Who is affected by this problem? Why does it matter? Ground this in your research from Modules 8-10 and the problem statement you developed in Module 11\.

#### **What I Built and Why**

Describe what you created and the reasoning behind your approach. What other directions did you consider? Why did you commit to this one? Connect your choices to your audience's needs and your own values.

#### **Key Design Decisions**

Walk the reader through 2-3 significant decisions you made during the project. For each decision, explain what options you weighed and why you chose the path you did. Show how your thinking evolved based on research, feedback, or constraints. After receiving peer feedback and instructor feedback, update this section with the feedback you received and how you addressed it. 

#### **Philosophical Frameworks That Shaped My Thinking**

Explain how course concepts informed your project. What foundational ethical frameworks did you notice from the Deep Research reports in Modules 8-10? In Modules 11-14, how did debates related to EA/X-risk, Nietzsche, Accelerationism, or Transhumanism help you think about bigger stakes? Finally, to what extent do these ethical concepts help you consider how different cultural or global perspectives might view your project differently? 

#### **Working with AI as a Partner**

Describe how you used AI tools throughout this project. Be specific: What did you use AI for? How did you evaluate and refine its output? Where did you decide *not* to use AI, and why? Reflect on what this partnership taught you about working with AI effectively and ethically. Resource: review your notes from the Modules 11-14 AI Acknowledgements sections and present them as a narrative. 

#### **What I Learned**

Reflect on your growth through this project. What did you learn about applied AI ethics? What did you learn about yourself, your values, or your decision-making process? Based on this experience, what would you do differently next time?

**Format:** .pdf or .docx
